Data Types

Reading: Types of Variables

Data Types

and numerical discrete!

Categorical Nominal

  • Name only, qualitative
    • car makes
    • zip code
    • eye color
  • Dichotomous variable refers to a categorical nominal variables with only two values
    • left handed / right handed
    • current student / non student

Categorical Ordinal

  • ordered, quasi-quantitative
    • agreement level
    • education level
  • ordinal data can seem almost quantitative, but there is no way to know the value of the levels
  • ordinal scales are usually used to measure non-numeric features like happiness, customer satisfaction
  • Likert Items are often used to obtain categorical ordinal data

Likert Items

Numerical Data

  • Numerical data are quantitative
  • Discrete means countable
    • number of students
    • number of views or likes

Numerical Data

  • Numerical data are quantitative
  • Continuous means measured
    • height must be measured
    • Interval refers to continuous data with no reference point
      • temperature, we know 28 F vs 33 F is a difference of 5, but does a difference of 5 mean the same thing between 100 and 105?
      • Interval data cannot be divided meaningfully. Don’t compute what is 1/5 of the temperature today
      • Stats use division, causing a problem for interval data
    • Ratio refers to continuous data with a reference point
      • Height, weight, distance, speed
      • division or other ratios are meaningful. Professor Luman is twice my height!

Data Acquisition

Getting brand new data using surveys and sensors!

Surveys for Acquisition

  • surveys are directed toward people
  • surveys answer subjective perceptual and behavioral questions, primarily
  • different questions produce different data types
    • multiple choice questions
    • free response questions
    • slider ratings
    • Likert scales

Surveys for Acquisition

  • Survey development should be guided by a hypothesis
  • Only questions that have appropriate data types for analysis should be used
  • Quality controls should be in place (e.g. timestamps, inclusion criteria)
  • Data security protocols should be in place to protect private data
  • Human Subjects Research must be reviewed by the Institutional Review Board

Reading: Surveys

Sensors for Acquisition

  • sensors can measure inanimate objects/materials or people/animals
  • electronic sensors for things
    • motion detection
    • freezer thermometer
    • tire pressure sensor
  • sensors for people
    • motion capture
    • body temperature
    • electro-physiological
      • ECG - electrocardiogram
      • EEG - electroencephalogram
      • electrodermal

Sensors for Acquisition

Acquisition Drawbacks

  • time consuming
  • data management is required (persistent storage, security)
  • maybe the data already exist somewhere
  • maybe surveys and sensors cannot answer the question you have

Extracted Data

Extracting Data from Documents

  • Documents often contain information but it is not in an analyzable format
    • Author names on research papers
    • Foreground of an image
    • Background of an image
    • Video transcripts of speech
    • Video transcripts of sounds
    • Cells (biology) appearing in images
    • Language structure
      • noun, verb, adj, conjunction,
      • word length
      • num vowels
      • num syllables
      • etc

Extracting Data by Web Scraping

  • Scraping is gathering data form websites by reading into the html structure to get the content out of specific html tagged elements.
  • Scraping tools exist to assist with formatting
    • Requests, BeautifulSoup, Selenium

The forest itself is a <a href="/wiki/Chinju_no_Mori" title="Chinju no Mori">sacred grove</a> associated with an important <a href="/wiki/Shinto" title="Shinto">Shinto</a> sanctuary complex known in Japanese as the <a href="/wiki/Kamo_Shrine" class="mw-redirect" title="Kamo Shrine">Kamo-<i>jinja</i></a>, situated near the banks of the <a href="/wiki/Kamo_River" title="Kamo River">Kamo River</a> just north of where the <a href="/wiki/Takano_River" title="Takano River">Takano River</a> joins the Kamo River in northeast <a href="/wiki/Kyoto" title="Kyoto">Kyoto</a> city, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>. The term Kamo-<i>jinja</i> in Japanese is a general reference to <a href="/wiki/Shimogamo_Shrine" title="Shimogamo Shrine">Shimogamo Shrine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kamigamo_Shrine" title="Kamigamo Shrine">Kamigamo Shrine</a>, the traditionally linked Kamo shrines of Kyoto.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The Kamo<i>-jinja</i> serve the function of protecting Kyoto from malign influences.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>Positioned at the convergence of the Kamo River and Takano River, the area of Tadasu No Mori\'s virgin forest encompasses about 12.4 hectares, or 124,500 square meters (more than double the size of Liberty Island where the Statue of Liberty stands).\n</p>\n<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Historical_overview">Historical overview</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tadasu_no_Mori&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Historical overview"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>The preserved forest of Tadasu No Mori lies within the Shimogamo Shrine area, one of the seventeen historical sites in and around <a href="/wiki/Kyoto" title="Kyoto">Kyoto</a> which in 1994 were designated by UNESCO as <a href="/wiki/Historic_Monuments_of_Ancient_Kyoto" class="mw-redirect" title="Historic Monuments of Ancient Kyoto">Historic Monuments of Ancient Kyoto</a>. It is today the last remnant of a <a href="/wiki/Primeval_forest" class="mw-redirect" title="Primeval forest">primeval forest</a> which is reputed to have never been burned down. The forest has, in fact, suffered some damage over the centuries when all of Kyoto was burned during successive revolts and wars, but the forest growth has rebounded again and again. The forest is left to grow in its natural state. It is neither planted nor pruned.\n</p><p>Originally in Ancient Kyoto, the forest spanned about 4.95 million square meters, however, Kyoto was the stage for multiple wars during the middle ages of Japan, including the Onin War (1467-1477). On June 14 1470, during the Onin War, 70% of Tadasu No Mori was burned to the ground. Additionally, some areas of the forest were then taken by the Shogunate via the land forfeit ordinances in the 4th year of the <a href="/wiki/Meiji_era" title="Meiji era">Meiji era</a>, reducing the territory of Tadasu No Mori to its current size.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>\nSince the period in which it was a part of the Yamashiro Province, the flora of Tadasu No Mori, the core of which is deciduous elm trees including the zelkova and the hackberry (nettle), has been largely maintained, totaling about 4,700 trees of 40 distinct species. The forest spans from the Kamo River to the Takano River upon a narrow strip of land running north-south between the two waterways. Many clear-water streams flow throughout the forest and feed into the two larger rivers, while the outskirts of the forest are also dense with vegetation which thrives along the riverside. Mentioned in "Tale of Genji" and "The Pillow Book" of ancient times, the forest continues to be a historic landmark, to this day offering a refuge of rest and relaxation.</p><figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-right" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Tadasu_no_mori_200906d.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Tadasu_no_mori_200906d.jpg/220px-Tadasu_no_mori_200906d.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="293" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Tadasu_no_mori_200906d.jpg/330px-Tadasu_no_mori_200906d.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Tadasu_no_mori_200906d.jpg/440px-Tadasu_no_mori_200906d.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1368" data-file-height="1824" /></a><figcaption>A tree singled out from amongst the others in the forest</figcaption></figure>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="World_Heritage_site">World Heritage site</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tadasu_no_Mori&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: World Heritage site"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>In 1983, Tadasu No Mori was designated as a National Historic Site, protecting it as a site of ecological preservation. Then in 1994, Tadasu No Mori became a World Heritage site, as the entirety of the <a href="/wiki/Shimogamo_Shrine" title="Shimogamo Shrine">Shimogamo Shrine</a> grounds, which encompasses the forest, was designated by UNESCO as a <a href="/wiki/Historic_Monuments_of_Ancient_Kyoto" class="mw-redirect" title="Historic Monuments of Ancient Kyoto">Historic Monuments of Ancient Kyoto</a>, along with seventeen other sites in the Kyoto area.\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Ecology">Ecology</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tadasu_no_Mori&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ecology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>Tadasu No Mori is referred to as an untouched virgin forest, but in reality, lots of human effort has been made to preserve the forest over the years. Tadasu No Mori is composed mostly of deciduous tree species, especially those commonly found along riversides. Additionally, many camphor trees were planted after the Muroto Typhoon of 1934 caused severe flooding which drowned much of the forest\'s vegetation. Other plants such as the red camellia were later planted to aid the function and aesthetic of the Shimogamo Shrine. In contrast to the generally dark and mysterious atmosphere of some shrines, the area of Tadasu No Mori is rather bright as there is not a dense presence of coniferous trees.\n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Climate">Climate</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tadasu_no_Mori&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Climate"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>As the Kyoto Basin sits at a warm temperate zone of Japan, broad-leaf coniferous forests commonly develop in this region. During winter months, however, Tadasu No Mori, located at the northern most border of the Kyoto Basin, becomes far colder at night, making conditions unsuitable for the growth of conifers. Instead, broad-leaf deciduous forests grow strongly, particularly those of the Elm family.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> \n</p>\n<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Trees">Trees</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tadasu_no_Mori&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Trees"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>Today, Tadasu No Mori is host to about mature 4,700 trees of diameter greater than 10 centimeters. In the near future, there will be about 600 trees within Tadasu No Mori between the ages of 200 and 600 years-old. class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Wildlife">Wildlife</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tadasu_no_Mori&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Wildlife"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>\n<p>Not many large mammals have been observed in Tadasu No Mori. In the 1960s there were sightings of the Japanese Squirrel (Sciurus lis), however, in recent years the squirrel\'s presence has vanished. In terms of birds, the Brown Hawk-Owl (Ninox scutulata), a migratory bird which generally spends winters in South East Asia, was observed on three instances nesting within hollow trees during a 2002 survey of Tadasu No Mori.
